# Product

## Register

product

## Users

University students (typically 18–24), using StudentOS solo on iPad and Mac desktop. They're managing multiple subjects simultaneously during an active semester: tracking lectures and labs, staying on top of assignments and exams, studying with focus, and keeping personal todos alongside academic obligations.

They open this app during stressful, high-stakes periods — the week before exams, the morning an assignment is due. The primary job is reducing cognitive load when it's highest.

## Product Purpose

StudentOS organises a university student's entire semester in one personal tool. It connects their subjects, schedule, calendar, notes, study sessions, todos, and grade tracking into a single coherent space that understands their academic context — teaching weeks, break periods, assessment weights, domain structure.

Success means the student spends less time managing their study life and more time actually studying. The app should feel indispensable rather than optional by week two of semester.

## Brand Personality

Personal, warm, calm.

The voice is considered and direct — no cheerleading, no artificial urgency. It speaks like a well-organised friend who already knows your semester, not a corporate tool that needs you to configure it.

## Reference

Arc Browser: opinionated without being loud, premium dark surfaces, delightful details that reward attention without demanding it, feels like a space you made your own. The key quality to borrow is that Arc has a point of view — it made choices the user comes to trust — rather than a blank-slate configurability.

## Anti-references

- Generic SaaS dashboard: blue sidebar, white cards, metric tiles, stock illustrations, identical card grids. If it could be a CRM it's wrong.
- Gamified student apps: XP bars, streaks, bright toy colors, achievement badges. Motivation through guilt or dopamine mechanics is the opposite of calm.
- Google/Microsoft productivity: flat, corporate, interchangeable, no personality. Correct but forgettable.
- Aesthetic-only: looks polished in a screenshot, frustrating to use for 30 minutes. Design must serve the task, not perform for an audience.

## Design Principles

1. **Yours, not ours.** The app should feel like the student's own space — shaped around their subjects, their semester, their rhythm. Every surface should feel inhabited, not generic.
2. **Calm at the centre.** The primary user state is stressed. Motion, color, and hierarchy should reduce cognitive load, not add to it. Nothing should demand attention it hasn't earned.
3. **Delight without noise.** Surprising, considered details are welcome. Animations that announce themselves, badges that celebrate nothing, colors that perform — these are not.
4. **Considered over correct.** Every flow should feel like someone thought about it specifically for a university student, not borrowed from a generic productivity template. Opinionated is right; configurable-for-all is wrong.
5. **Personal scale.** This is a solo tool, not a platform. Optimise for one student's experience in depth. Don't abstract toward hypothetical multi-user or enterprise use.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA as a reasonable baseline: 4.5:1 contrast on body text, keyboard navigable core flows, `prefers-reduced-motion` respected on all animations. Not strictly enforced across every state, but contrast and motion are non-negotiable given students may use the app for extended periods under low-light conditions.
